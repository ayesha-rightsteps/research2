# Background: A Multi-Agent Resource Allocation Method for Unmanned Aerial Vehicle Networks based on Adaptive Spatial Reward Mechanisms and Context-Awareness

## What field does this paper belong to?

This paper belongs to the intersection of wireless communications engineering and machine learning, specifically the subfield of multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) applied to UAV (Unmanned Aerial Vehicle) communication network resource management. It was presented at the 2026 5th International Conference on Big Data, Information and Computer Network (BDICN 2026) and addresses challenges in dynamic spectrum and power resource allocation for aerial base station networks.

## The Evolution of This Problem

Wireless communication has always faced a resource scarcity problem: a finite amount of spectrum and power must be shared among a growing number of users. In traditional terrestrial cellular networks, a central base station manages these resources with full knowledge of the network environment. This centralized approach works when infrastructure is fixed and the environment is relatively predictable, but it faces severe limitations when the network nodes themselves are mobile and battery-constrained — precisely the situation with UAVs.

UAVs attracted significant attention in the 2010s as a way to rapidly deploy communication coverage in areas where ground infrastructure is absent or damaged. Because UAV-to-ground links tend to exhibit Line-of-Sight (LoS) characteristics — a much cleaner signal path than the multipath-cluttered terrestrial channels — UAVs can serve as effective aerial base stations. Early research focused on where to place a single UAV in three dimensions to maximize the coverage area for ground users, with seminal work by Mozaffari and Saad proposing 3D deployment based on circular packing principles.

As single-UAV deployment was well understood, the field shifted to multi-UAV systems and trajectory design. Researchers examined how fleets of UAVs could coordinate flight paths to maximize throughput or minimize task completion time. However, these early works typically assumed a centralized controller with full access to all channel state information (CSI), which is impractical: UAVs have limited communication bandwidth with a ground controller, they are continuously moving, and the wireless environment changes dynamically with their position. Furthermore, centralized systems scale poorly — as the number of UAVs increases, the computational burden on the central controller grows combinatorially.

This scalability and information bottleneck problem prompted the adoption of Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning. In a MARL framework, each UAV is treated as an independent agent that learns its own resource allocation policy through experience — choosing which sub-channel to use, which ground user to serve, and at what power level — without needing to report its full state to a central node. While MARL had already been applied in cognitive radio and device-to-device (D2D) networks, its application to multi-UAV systems with simultaneous user selection, subchannel allocation, and power control remained sparse. Existing MARL approaches for UAVs often suffered from slow convergence and poor performance in dense, high-user-count environments because they either ignored inter-agent spatial relationships or failed to share useful context information between neighboring agents in real time.

## Key Technologies This Paper Builds On

### Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) with Actor-Critic Architecture
MARL is a framework where multiple autonomous agents each learn a policy by interacting with a shared environment and receiving individual rewards. This paper builds on the Advantage Actor-Critic (A2C) variant, where each agent maintains both an actor network (which selects actions) and a critic network (which estimates the value of states to guide policy improvement). The paper draws directly on prior MARL work in UAV networks (Dai et al. 2022), cognitive radio networks, and the foundational MADDPG algorithm (Lowe et al. 2017), and treats each UAV as a fully independent agent that learns using only local observations and neighbor communications.

### UAV Air-to-Ground Channel Modeling
The paper models two channel types that are standard in UAV communication research: the probabilistic LoS/NLoS model, which captures the statistical likelihood of line-of-sight propagation depending on altitude and environment (with parameters for urban vs. rural environments), and the deterministic LoS free-space path loss model. Both models quantify path loss and channel gain as a function of the UAV's 3D position relative to ground users, forming the physical basis for all SINR and throughput calculations in the learning environment.

### Deep Q-Networks (DQN) and LSTM Encoders
Each agent's policy learning uses a Deep Q-Network to approximate Q-values for discrete action spaces (user selection, subchannel selection, power level). For the context-aware communication mechanism, a 64-neuron LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) encoder is used to fuse an agent's own state encoding with compressed hidden states received from neighboring agents, allowing agents to integrate temporal history and spatial neighborhood context into a single hidden representation.

## Why This Paper's Approach is Different

The paper's core innovation is the ASAC (Adaptive Spatial Reward and Agent Context-Awareness) framework, which addresses two distinct failure modes in standard MARL for UAV networks. First, standard global reward sharing treats all neighbor agents equally regardless of distance, causing distant agents to contaminate the reward signal with irrelevant information. ASAC introduces a spatial discount factor (kappa) that geometrically reduces the weight of rewards from agents farther away, so nearby agents' outcomes dominate local policy updates. This makes the reward landscape smoother and convergence faster.

Second, standard non-communicating MARL agents (like IA2C) are blind to their neighbors' current strategies. ASAC adds a context-aware communication mechanism in which each agent encodes its hidden state and broadcasts it to neighbors at each time step. An LSTM fuses this incoming context with the agent's own current observation, giving each agent a richer view of the local network state. An Exponential Moving Average (EMA) action smoothing layer further reduces the randomness of sampled actions during training. Together, these two mechanisms achieve a 16% improvement in resource allocation efficiency over the best baseline (MAB) in high-user-count scenarios, while also converging faster across both LoS and probabilistic channel models.

## Where Does This Paper Sit in the Bigger Picture?

This paper sits at the convergence of two growing trends: the deployment of UAV swarms as dynamic network infrastructure, and the push toward fully distributed, AI-driven network management. It directly addresses the gap between idealized centralized resource management (which assumes omniscient controllers) and the practical reality of distributed UAV fleets with limited communication and computation. The ASAC framework is a step toward self-organizing aerial networks where each UAV autonomously learns context-aware resource allocation policies, enabling reliable connectivity in disaster response, temporary event coverage, and military applications where ground infrastructure is unavailable. The work also points forward to open problems: how to handle UAV mobility in trajectory-coupled resource allocation, how to scale the context-sharing mechanism to very large fleets, and how to extend the model to heterogeneous UAV types with different hardware constraints.
