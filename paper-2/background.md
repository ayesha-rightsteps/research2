# Background: Lyapunov-Guided Diffusion-Based DRL for UAV-Assisted Vehicular Networks

## Field

This paper sits at the intersection of wireless communications, intelligent transportation systems (ITS), and deep reinforcement learning (DRL). More specifically, it belongs to the subfield of UAV-assisted vehicle-to-everything (V2X) networking, where unmanned aerial vehicles act as mobile aerial base stations to serve highly dynamic vehicular environments. The applied machine learning contribution centers on diffusion model-based policy optimization within the DRL framework.

## Evolution of the Problem: A Narrative of the Field

**The V2X connectivity problem.** Intelligent transportation systems depend on reliable, low-latency communication between vehicles and infrastructure. Traditional vehicle-to-infrastructure (V2I) links — fixed base stations and roadside units — break down in two important situations: urban areas with dense traffic, where competition for spectrum becomes severe, and remote or cross-border highways, where deploying fixed infrastructure is economically infeasible. Vehicle-to-vehicle (V2V) links fill some of the gap but suffer from Doppler shift and frequent blockages caused by high vehicle mobility. Neither paradigm alone is sufficient to guarantee seamless connectivity for safety-critical or high-throughput applications.

**UAVs as aerial base stations.** Researchers recognized that unmanned aerial vehicles could complement ground infrastructure by acting as flexible, mobile relay nodes or base stations. Because UAVs fly above obstructions, they enjoy a high probability of establishing line-of-sight (LoS) connections with ground vehicles. Their mobility is a further advantage: a UAV can physically track a cluster of moving vehicles, something a roadside unit cannot do. Early UAV-assisted communication research focused on static ground users, jointly optimizing UAV trajectories and resource allocation using convex optimization or heuristic search. When the focus shifted to vehicular networks, the dynamic nature of the problem — vehicles moving at highway speeds, rapidly changing channels, spectrum shared between V2U and V2V links — pushed classical methods to their limits.

**Deep reinforcement learning enters the picture.** Convex optimization requires problem convexity and full knowledge of future states. Heuristic algorithms converge slowly and cannot guarantee real-time decisions. DRL offered a fundamentally different approach: learn a policy that maps observed system states (channel conditions, vehicle positions, UAV energy status) to actions (channel allocation, transmit power, flight altitude) through repeated interaction with the environment. Early DRL applications to UAV networking used deep Q-networks (DQN) or deep deterministic policy gradient (DDPG) with multi-layer perceptron (MLP) actor networks. These methods brought the problem into the realm of real-time adaptive control, but they exposed new limitations: MLP-based actors are prone to getting stuck in local optima, the exploration-exploitation trade-off is difficult to manage, and neither DQN nor DDPG was designed to handle the long-horizon energy constraint imposed by a UAV's finite battery life.

**Lyapunov optimization as a bridge between long-term and short-term.** The UAV's energy constraint is fundamentally a long-term, stochastic constraint: energy consumed across hundreds of time slots must not exceed a threshold, but the UAV cannot know future channel states or vehicle positions in advance. Lyapunov optimization provides a mathematically principled way to convert this multi-stage constraint into a per-slot deterministic penalty. By maintaining a virtual queue that tracks accumulated energy overshoot, the technique allows the DRL agent to focus on a single time slot at a time while still respecting the long-run energy budget — no prediction of future dynamics is needed.

**Diffusion models: from image generation to decision-making.** The most recent development is the adoption of denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs), originally developed for high-quality image synthesis, as policy networks inside DRL agents. A diffusion model generates outputs through an iterative denoising process: starting from pure Gaussian noise and progressively removing noise over several steps guided by a learned denoiser. When the "image" being generated is a vector of resource allocation decisions (channel assignments, power levels, altitude adjustments), the iterative nature of the diffusion process provides structured exploration that MLP-based actors lack, improving both the quality of the final action and robustness to noisy or delayed inputs such as CSI with feedback delay.

## Key Technologies

- **Lyapunov optimization**: converts a long-term stochastic constraint into per-slot deterministic subproblems via virtual queues and a drift-plus-penalty function.
- **Diffusion models (DDPM)**: generative models that produce outputs by iteratively denoising Gaussian noise, used here as the actor network in DRL.
- **DDPG (Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient)**: an actor-critic DRL algorithm for continuous action spaces; the backbone on which D3PG is built.
- **UAV-assisted vehicular networks**: system architecture where a UAV serves as an aerial base station, jointly managing V2U and V2V links over a shared spectrum.

## Why This Paper's Approach is Different

Most prior work on UAV-assisted vehicular networks either ignores the UAV's long-term energy constraint or assumes perfect, instantaneous CSI — both assumptions that break down in practical highway deployments. This paper addresses both gaps simultaneously. The Lyapunov layer ensures the energy budget is respected without any look-ahead into future dynamics. The diffusion model actor replaces the standard MLP, giving the agent a richer, multi-step mechanism for exploring the combined discrete-and-continuous action space (channel allocation is binary; power control and altitude adjustment are continuous). The paper also grounds its evaluation in reality: vehicle mobility traces come from a real Xiamen highway imported into OpenStreetMap and simulated in SUMO, rather than idealized synthetic scenarios.

## Where It Sits in the Bigger Picture

This paper represents a convergence of three active research streams — UAV networking, Lyapunov-based resource management, and diffusion-model-augmented DRL — applied to the emerging low-altitude economy. It demonstrates that generative AI techniques developed for creative tasks (image synthesis) can directly improve the robustness and performance of wireless resource allocation policies. The framework is extensible: the single-UAV assumption can be relaxed to multi-UAV coverage by partitioning the highway, and the diffusion-based actor architecture is applicable to any DRL problem requiring exploration in high-dimensional mixed action spaces.
