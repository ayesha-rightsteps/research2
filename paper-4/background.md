# Background: Joint Optimization of Trajectory Control, Resource Allocation, and Task Offloading for Multi-UAV-Assisted IoV

## What field does this paper belong to?

This paper sits at the intersection of **wireless communications**, **mobile edge computing (MEC)**, and **intelligent transportation systems**. More specifically, it contributes to the sub-field of **UAV-assisted MEC for Internet of Vehicles (IoV)** — a branch of research concerned with how drone-mounted computing servers can help cars and trucks offload data-intensive tasks in dense urban environments. The work draws simultaneously on optimization theory (convex programming), machine learning (deep reinforcement learning), and natural language processing (large language models), making it an interdisciplinary systems paper.

## The Evolution of This Problem

The story begins with classical cellular infrastructure. In early vehicular networks, cars communicated with roadside units (RSUs) or ground base stations (BSs) through Vehicle-to-Infrastructure (V2I) links, and exchanged safety data among themselves via Vehicle-to-Vehicle (V2V) links. As long as traffic remained sparse and tasks were simple, on-board computing was sufficient. However, the explosive growth of sensor data — LiDAR point clouds, HD maps, real-time video — made it impossible for on-board units (OBUs) to process everything locally within tight deadlines.

Mobile Edge Computing (MEC) emerged as the first serious answer to this problem. By offloading computation from vehicles to edge servers co-located with RSUs or BSs, MEC dramatically reduced latency compared to cloud offloading. Ground-based MEC worked well in controlled conditions, but urban reality exposed two structural weaknesses: dense buildings block line-of-sight links between fixed BSs and moving vehicles, causing severe signal attenuation; and during peak hours, static ground infrastructure simply runs out of capacity when hundreds of vehicles flood a single intersection with offloading requests simultaneously.

UAV-assisted MEC appeared as the next evolution. A UAV flying at low altitude above a street can establish high-probability line-of-sight (LoS) links with vehicles below, bypassing the building-blockage problem entirely. More importantly, a UAV can fly toward where vehicles are densest, acting as a mobile edge server that dynamically repositions itself — something a fixed BS can never do. Early UAV-MEC studies showed significant gains in both latency and task success rates. However, most of these works assumed a single UAV, fixed altitude, horizontal-only movement, or simplified communication models that ignored how altitude variation changes the LoS probability and coverage radius in real time.

Multi-UAV coordination raised the complexity significantly. With several UAVs operating in the same airspace, the system must avoid mid-air collisions, prevent redundant coverage of the same vehicles, balance computational loads across the swarm, and jointly decide how each vehicle should split its task among local computation, UAV offloading, and BS offloading. These decisions are tightly coupled: the best offloading split depends on the communication rates, which depend on UAV positions, which depend on the trajectory optimizer, which in turn depends on knowing which vehicles need service. Prior multi-UAV studies either solved only part of this problem (trajectory only, or resource scheduling only) or relied exclusively on multi-agent DRL, which is opaque and generalizes poorly to scenarios not seen during training.

This paper represents the current frontier: a **fully joint, three-dimensional optimization** that simultaneously addresses trajectory control (including altitude dynamics), resource block and power allocation, and continuous task offloading ratios, all in a dense urban IoV scenario. Its distinguishing contribution is integrating LLMs as interpretable semantic reasoners on top of the DRL-based resource scheduler, specifically to rescue the long-tail failure cases that DRL handles poorly. The combination of SOCP for trajectory, DRL+LLM for resource scheduling, and LP for offloading ratios is the paper's core architectural novelty.

## Key Technologies This Paper Builds On

### Mobile Edge Computing (MEC) and Task Offloading
MEC is the practice of placing computing servers at the network edge — close to where data is generated — rather than in a distant cloud. In IoV, vehicles offload computational tasks (e.g., object detection, route planning) to edge servers at RSUs, BSs, or UAVs to meet strict latency deadlines. Task offloading is the act of deciding how much computation to send where: a vehicle can process some fraction locally (on its OBU), send another fraction to a UAV via a ground-to-air (G2A) link, and send the remainder to the ground BS via a V2I link. The optimal split is a continuous ratio problem that changes every time slot.

### Second-Order Cone Programming (SOCP)
SOCP is a subclass of convex optimization where some constraints take the form of second-order cones — norms bounded by a linear expression. SOCP problems are polynomial-time solvable by interior-point methods and are widely used in antenna placement, beamforming, and trajectory planning. In this paper, the authors reformulate the non-convex UAV trajectory optimization problem (coverage radius is a nonlinear function of altitude; collision avoidance is a non-convex exterior region) into a standard SOCP by applying Taylor approximations for energy costs and tangent-line relaxations for collision avoidance. This makes each UAV's per-slot trajectory update fast and provably convergent.

### Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) and DDPG
DRL trains an agent to choose actions in an environment by maximizing cumulative reward through trial-and-error interaction. For resource scheduling — assigning resource blocks (RBs) and transmit power to each vehicle — the action space is high-dimensional, mixed discrete-continuous, and too complex for classical optimization. The Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG) algorithm, which uses Actor-Critic neural networks with a replay buffer and target networks, handles continuous action spaces and is the DRL backbone here. Its limitation is poor generalization to situations outside its training distribution — which is where the LLM layer comes in.

### Large Language Models (LLMs) as Agents
LLMs are neural networks pre-trained on internet-scale text corpora that have developed strong general reasoning, analogy, and instruction-following capabilities. Recent research has shown that LLMs can reason about physical systems and communications networks without task-specific fine-tuning, using in-context learning (ICL) — providing examples and rules directly in the prompt. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting further enables LLMs to produce explicit reasoning steps, making their decisions interpretable. This paper uses an LLM (specifically Qwen3-235B-A22B) as a macro-scheduler: given structured JSON descriptions of failed tasks and surplus tasks produced by the DRL agent, the LLM reasons about which resource blocks to transfer and which power levels to adjust, producing both actions and natural-language justifications.

### Linear Programming (LP) and Alternating Optimization
LP is the classical framework for optimizing a linear objective subject to linear inequality and equality constraints. It finds globally optimal solutions in polynomial time. After fixing UAV positions and communication resources (from SOCP and DRL+LLM respectively), the task offloading ratios become the only unknowns. By substituting historical average queuing delays as constants, the paper converts the originally non-convex problem into a standard LP that can be solved exactly and quickly. Alternating optimization refers to the overall strategy of cycling between fixing one set of variables and optimizing another, repeating until convergence — the same logic that makes coordinate descent effective.

## Why This Paper's Approach is Different

Previous studies either (a) optimized trajectory separately from resource scheduling, (b) used multi-agent DRL alone (suffering from black-box opacity and poor generalization), (c) ignored 3D altitude variation and its effect on coverage, or (d) assumed fixed user positions incompatible with dynamic IoV. This paper is the **first to deploy an LLM as a macro-scheduler in a UAV-IoV system specifically to handle long-tail task failures** — the cases where DRL assigns too few resources to some vehicles because they appear rarely in training. By introducing the reward decoupling mechanism (the DRL's training signal is calculated from the DRL's own action, not the LLM's revised action), the paper also solves the interference problem that would otherwise corrupt DRL policy learning when an external corrector modifies the allocation. The result is a system that combines mathematical guarantees (SOCP convergence, LP global optimality) with learned heuristics (DRL) and semantic reasoning (LLM), a genuinely hybrid approach not seen in prior UAV-MEC literature.

## Where Does This Paper Sit in the Bigger Picture?

This work contributes to at least three broader research trends. First, it advances the **integration of AI-native methods into wireless network optimization**, following the 6G vision of networks that reason about themselves. Second, it is part of the growing body of work on **LLMs as network management agents**, alongside papers on LLM-assisted power control and beamforming — demonstrating that pre-trained language models can substitute for domain-specific heuristics even in technical engineering domains. Third, it pushes the frontier of **joint optimization under coupling constraints**, showing that decomposing a tightly coupled non-convex problem into specialized sub-solvers (SOCP, DRL, LP) connected by alternating optimization is both tractable and high-performing. The open-source code and prompt release make the work directly extensible by the research community. Near-term follow-on work could include multi-hop UAV relaying, energy harvesting, or replacing the DDPG backbone with transformer-based DRL architectures.
