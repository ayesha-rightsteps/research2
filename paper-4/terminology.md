# Terminology: Joint Optimization of Trajectory Control, Resource Allocation, and Task Offloading for Multi-UAV-Assisted IoV

> Quick reference for all technical terms used in this paper. The 5 most central terms are marked ⭐. Terms are grouped by theme.

---

## Core System Components

### UAV (Unmanned Aerial Vehicle)
**What it is:** A drone — a remotely piloted or autonomous aircraft with no pilot on board. In this paper, UAVs are small rotary-wing aircraft (like quadcopters or hexacopters) flying at low altitude (50–100 m) above city streets.
**In this paper:** Each UAV carries an edge computing server and acts as a flying base station. The system has U = 5 UAVs operating simultaneously. They are the central subjects of the trajectory optimization problem.
**Easy way to remember:** Think of a taxi drone that also has a laptop strapped to it — it flies to where customers are and does their computing on the spot.

### MEC (Mobile Edge Computing) ⭐
**What it is:** A network architecture where computing servers are placed at the edge of the network — physically close to end users — rather than in a remote data center. This dramatically cuts the round-trip latency for offloaded tasks.
**In this paper:** MEC is the fundamental motivation for the whole system. UAVs act as aerial MEC nodes. Vehicles offload their computationally intensive tasks to UAV-mounted or BS-mounted MEC servers to meet strict 1-second deadlines.
**Easy way to remember:** Instead of mailing your homework to a data center in another city, you hand it to a tutor standing next to you — that's MEC.

### IoV (Internet of Vehicles)
**What it is:** An extension of the Internet of Things (IoT) concept applied to transportation. Vehicles are connected nodes that communicate with each other (V2V), with infrastructure (V2I), and with network services to enable intelligent transportation.
**In this paper:** IoV defines the scenario context. Vehicles in a 300m x 300m urban grid need to offload computational tasks generated by sensors, cameras, and navigation systems. The paper assumes 50 vehicles whose tasks arrive stochastically each time slot.
**Easy way to remember:** The Internet for cars — every vehicle is a connected device with data needs and computing limits.

### BS (Base Station)
**What it is:** A fixed ground infrastructure tower that provides wireless connectivity to mobile devices. In 5G, base stations use NR-V2X standards for vehicle communications.
**In this paper:** A single BS is located at the center of the 300m x 300m scenario. It handles all V2I communication and has the highest computing frequency (9 GHz) but serves all vehicles simultaneously, creating queuing delays during peak load.
**Easy way to remember:** The fixed cell tower that every car can always reach, but that gets congested when too many cars try to use it at once.

### RSU (Roadside Unit)
**What it is:** Fixed infrastructure units placed along roadsides that communicate directly with nearby vehicles, providing both connectivity and sometimes local computing.
**In this paper:** RSUs are mentioned as part of the broader IoV reference context (vehicles communicate with RSUs or BSs for V2I). The paper's own system model uses a single central BS rather than multiple RSUs.
**Easy way to remember:** Like a Wi-Fi hotspot mounted on a streetlight that talks to passing cars.

### OBU (On-Board Unit)
**What it is:** The computing and communication hardware installed in a vehicle — essentially the vehicle's on-board computer that handles sensors, communication radios, and local processing.
**In this paper:** All vehicles have identical OBUs with computing frequency fm = 0.5 GHz. Tasks that are not offloaded are computed locally on the OBU. This frequency is far lower than UAV (5 GHz) or BS (9 GHz) servers, making offloading attractive for large tasks.
**Easy way to remember:** The car's built-in laptop — fast enough for simple things, but overwhelmed by heavy tasks.

## Communication Links and Propagation

### V2I (Vehicle-to-Infrastructure)
**What it is:** Wireless communication between a vehicle and fixed infrastructure (BS or RSU). Uses cellular standards (5G NR-V2X in this paper).
**In this paper:** Every vehicle in the scenario can communicate with the BS via V2I at all times. V2I links experience free-space path loss, shadow fading, and fast Rayleigh fading. The data rate depends on allocated bandwidth and received signal power.
**Easy way to remember:** The car talking to the cell tower — always available but shared among many users.

### V2V (Vehicle-to-Vehicle)
**What it is:** Direct wireless communication between two vehicles without going through any infrastructure. Used for cooperative safety applications (collision warnings, platooning).
**In this paper:** Mentioned in the introduction as part of IoV context but not modeled in the paper's own system. The paper focuses on V2I and V2U (vehicle-to-UAV) links.
**Easy way to remember:** Two cars whispering directly to each other — no middleman needed.

### G2A (Ground-to-Air)
**What it is:** Wireless link from a ground device (here, a vehicle) upward to an airborne platform (the UAV). Sometimes called the access link in UAV communications.
**In this paper:** The G2A link is modeled with a probabilistic LoS/NLoS channel. The LoS probability depends on the elevation angle between the vehicle and the UAV — the higher the UAV flies directly above the vehicle, the better the LoS probability, but higher altitude also means greater free-space path loss.
**Easy way to remember:** A car sending a Wi-Fi signal up to a drone overhead.

### LoS (Line of Sight) ⭐
**What it is:** A propagation condition where the transmitter and receiver have an unobstructed direct path — no buildings or obstacles between them. LoS links are much stronger (10–20 dB better) than blocked NLoS links.
**In this paper:** UAVs flying above buildings can establish LoS links with vehicles more reliably than ground BSs can. The LoS probability is computed per vehicle-UAV pair using an elevation-angle-based formula with urban environment constants (ωa = 9.61, ωb = 0.16). This probability directly affects G2A channel quality and data rates.
**Easy way to remember:** Looking at someone directly across a room (LoS) vs. trying to talk to them through a wall (NLoS) — the direct path is always cleaner.

### NLoS (Non-Line of Sight)
**What it is:** A propagation condition where the signal must diffract or reflect around obstacles. NLoS links suffer additional path loss (ηNLoS = 20 dB in this paper) compared to LoS (ηLoS = 1 dB).
**In this paper:** The complementary condition to LoS. The average G2A path loss is a weighted combination of LoS and NLoS excess losses, weighted by their respective probabilities.
**Easy way to remember:** The signal has to bounce off walls to get where it's going — like light in a room with mirrors instead of windows.

### Resource Block (RB)
**What it is:** In 4G/5G, the smallest unit of radio spectrum that can be independently allocated to a user. Each RB has a fixed bandwidth (180 kHz in this paper) and occupies one time slot.
**In this paper:** RBs are the discrete resource units the DRL agent allocates to vehicles. Each vehicle can receive multiple RBs on its V2I and/or V2U links. More RBs = higher data rate = faster task offloading. The LLM macro-scheduler transfers RBs between vehicles to rescue failing tasks.
**Easy way to remember:** Like lanes on a highway — each car can be given one or more lanes, and more lanes means it can go faster.

## Optimization Methods

### SOCP (Second-Order Cone Programming) ⭐
**What it is:** A class of convex optimization problem where the constraints include second-order cones — sets of the form {(x, t) : ||x|| ≤ t}. SOCP is more general than LP and QP but less general than semidefinite programming, and can be solved in polynomial time by interior-point methods.
**In this paper:** The UAV 3D trajectory optimization subproblem is reformulated as a SOCP. Coverage radius constraints (which involve Euclidean norms) become SOC constraints. The collision avoidance constraint is linearized via a tangent line. The energy cost is quadratically approximated. The result is a standard SOCP solvable by cvxpy.
**Easy way to remember:** A mathematical toolkit for "find the best trajectory that satisfies these cone-shaped distance rules" — powerful enough to handle geometry but fast enough to run in real time.

### Second-Order Cone (SOC)
**What it is:** The geometric set defined by the constraint ||Ax + b|| ≤ c^T x + d — a cone-shaped feasible region in higher-dimensional space. Appears naturally when constraints involve Euclidean distances.
**In this paper:** The key coverage constraint — that a vehicle's horizontal distance from a UAV must be within the UAV's altitude-dependent coverage radius — is expressed as a SOC constraint after linear relaxation of integer coverage indicators.
**Easy way to remember:** If you draw all points within a certain distance from a point in 3D space, you get a sphere. Stack spheres of increasing size upward and you get a cone — that's the shape of these constraints.

### LP (Linear Programming)
**What it is:** Optimization of a linear objective function subject to linear inequality and equality constraints. LP finds the global optimum in polynomial time. Classic algorithms include the simplex method and interior-point methods.
**In this paper:** Once UAV trajectories and communication resources are fixed, the task offloading ratios (how much of each vehicle's task goes local, to UAV, or to BS) become the only decision variables. By substituting estimated queuing delays, the problem becomes a linear min-max problem solvable by LP. The paper uses Python's scipy linprog library.
**Easy way to remember:** If you've ever solved a "how many widgets to produce given limited materials" problem in school — that's LP. Here the "widgets" are fractions of computation tasks.

### Convex Optimization
**What it is:** A mathematical framework for minimizing a convex function over a convex set. Key property: any local minimum is also a global minimum. Includes LP, QP, SOCP, and SDP as special cases.
**In this paper:** The paper transforms the originally non-convex UAV trajectory problem into a convex one by approximating non-convex energy terms (Taylor expansion) and replacing non-convex collision avoidance regions with tangent-line linear constraints.
**Easy way to remember:** Optimizing a bowl-shaped problem — no matter where you start rolling downhill, you always end up at the same lowest point.

### Alternating Optimization
**What it is:** An iterative optimization strategy where multiple coupled variable blocks are optimized alternately — fix all variables except one block, optimize that block, then move to the next block, repeating until convergence. Also called Block Coordinate Descent (BCD).
**In this paper:** The paper uses alternating optimization at two levels. At the outer level, UAV trajectories and resource/offloading decisions alternate. At the inner level, resource scheduling (DRL+LLM) and task offloading (LP) alternate within each time slot in a "DRL → LP → LLM → LP" loop.
**Easy way to remember:** Like alternately adjusting the height and angle of a camera tripod — you can't adjust both at once perfectly, but if you keep going back and forth, you converge on the ideal setup.

## Machine Learning Methods

### DRL (Deep Reinforcement Learning) ⭐
**What it is:** A machine learning paradigm where an agent learns to take actions in an environment by receiving scalar rewards. "Deep" refers to using neural networks to represent the policy and/or value function. The agent learns through millions of simulated interactions.
**In this paper:** DRL (specifically DDPG) is the resource scheduler. The agent observes vehicle task loads, channel qualities, and UAV connection indicators, then outputs normalized power levels and scheduling priorities for each vehicle. It is trained offline and deployed to generate initial resource allocations each time slot.
**Easy way to remember:** Teaching a dog to do tricks through trial and error — good behavior gets treats (positive reward), bad behavior gets nothing. After many repetitions, the dog (agent) learns what to do.

### DDPG (Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient)
**What it is:** A DRL algorithm designed for continuous action spaces. It uses an Actor network (outputs actions deterministically) and a Critic network (evaluates action quality), along with experience replay and target networks for training stability.
**In this paper:** DDPG is the specific DRL algorithm used for resource scheduling. The Actor outputs normalized transmit power and scheduling priority per vehicle. The Critic evaluates the combined LP-computed cost as the reward signal.
**Easy way to remember:** A more sophisticated version of Q-learning that can handle "dial between 0 and 1" type actions instead of just "left, right, up, down."

### MADQN (Multi-Agent Deep Q-Network)
**What it is:** A multi-agent variant of Deep Q-Networks (DQN) where multiple agents each have their own Q-network and learn decentralized policies in a shared environment. DQN is limited to discrete action spaces.
**In this paper:** MADQN appears as a **baseline comparison** for the trajectory planning subproblem. It is not part of the proposed method. The paper shows that the SOCP-based approach outperforms MADQN for trajectory optimization.
**Easy way to remember:** Multiple agents each with their own brain, all playing the same video game and learning from their own scores.

### MADDPG (Multi-Agent Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient)
**What it is:** The multi-agent extension of DDPG, using centralized training with decentralized execution (CTDE). Each agent has its own Actor but all agents share a centralized Critic that can observe the global state.
**In this paper:** MADDPG appears as a **baseline comparison** for the task offloading subproblem. The paper argues that LP-based offloading outperforms MADDPG because LP guarantees global optimality for the offloading ratios given fixed resources.
**Easy way to remember:** Like DDPG but played by a whole team — training happens with a coach watching everything, but execution is each player acting on their own.

### LLM (Large Language Model) ⭐
**What it is:** A neural network, typically a transformer architecture, trained on massive corpora of text to predict the next token. When scaled to hundreds of billions of parameters, these models exhibit emergent reasoning, analogy, and planning capabilities.
**In this paper:** The LLM (Qwen3-235B-A22B) acts as a **macro-scheduler** that receives structured JSON descriptions of failed tasks and surplus tasks, then reasons about which resource blocks to transfer and what power levels to adjust. It operates with few-shot prompting (pre-written examples embedded in the prompt) and Chain-of-Thought reasoning, producing both actions and natural-language rationales.
**Easy way to remember:** A very well-read expert consultant who has never specifically trained on your company's network but can reason about it intelligently using general knowledge.

### Macro-Scheduler
**What it is:** A high-level controller that oversees a base-level allocator and makes corrective adjustments at a coarser granularity, focusing on systemic imbalances rather than per-packet decisions.
**In this paper:** The LLM plays the macro-scheduler role. Rather than replacing the DRL agent entirely, it monitors outcomes (task completion times) and intervenes only when DRL produces extreme cases: vehicles that fail to meet deadlines (failed tasks) or vehicles with abundant spare resources (surplus tasks). It then transfers resources from surplus to failed vehicles.
**Easy way to remember:** The shift supervisor who watches the assembly line (DRL workers) and reassigns people when one station gets overwhelmed — they don't do every task themselves, just manage the exceptions.

### Reward Decoupling
**What it is:** A mechanism to separate the learning signal used to train the DRL agent from the outcomes produced by an external corrector (the LLM). Without decoupling, LLM modifications to the allocation would corrupt the training signal, causing the DRL agent's policy to diverge.
**In this paper:** After the DRL agent produces its initial resource allocation, the system runs a first LP and records the cost as the DRL's reward — before the LLM makes any changes. The LLM then modifies the allocation, and a second LP is run to get the final offloading ratios. But the replay buffer stores the DRL reward (from LP 1), not the LLM-improved result (from LP 2). This ensures the DRL agent learns only from its own decisions.
**Easy way to remember:** You grade a student's test based on their original answers, not after a tutor corrected them — otherwise the student never learns from their own mistakes.

### In-Context Learning (ICL)
**What it is:** The ability of large language models to learn from examples provided directly in the prompt at inference time, without updating any model weights. The model generalizes from the provided pattern to new instances.
**In this paper:** The LLM uses few-shot ICL: high-quality scheduling examples generated offline by a large-scale LLM are embedded in the system prompt (Peg). The deployed edge LLM uses these examples to guide its decisions on new tasks, without any fine-tuning.
**Easy way to remember:** Showing someone three solved examples before asking them to solve a new problem — no formal training session, just learning from the sheet.

### Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting
**What it is:** A prompting technique that encourages LLMs to produce intermediate reasoning steps before their final answer, improving accuracy and making the reasoning process transparent.
**In this paper:** The LLM produces not just a list of actions (RB transfers, power adjustments) but also an "Analysis" field containing natural language explaining why each action was chosen. This is CoT — the reasoning trace is part of the output.
**Easy way to remember:** Asking someone to "show their work" on a math problem — the intermediate steps both improve the answer and let you understand how they got there.

### KV Caching (Key-Value Caching)
**What it is:** An inference optimization for transformer-based LLMs. The attention mechanism computes key and value matrices for each input token. KV caching stores these matrices for static (unchanging) parts of the prompt so they do not need to be recomputed on every inference call.
**In this paper:** The system prompt (Psys) and few-shot examples (Peg) are static and together make up the vast majority of the token count. Their KV cache is pre-computed once. Only the small dynamic data portion (Pdata, the JSON describing current failed and surplus tasks) needs fresh computation each call, drastically reducing inference latency.
**Easy way to remember:** Pre-computing the answers to questions you always ask before getting to the new question — like memorizing the multiplication table once instead of re-deriving it every time.

### MDP (Markov Decision Process)
**What it is:** A mathematical framework for sequential decision-making under uncertainty, consisting of: states (observations of the world), actions (choices), transition dynamics, and rewards. The "Markov" property means the next state depends only on the current state and action, not the full history.
**In this paper:** The DRL resource scheduling problem is formulated as an MDP. The state is each vehicle's task load and channel conditions; the action is power and priority for each vehicle; the reward is the negative total cost from the LP solution.
**Easy way to remember:** A formalized version of "what do I do right now, given where I am, to earn the most points in the long run?"

## System Architecture Terms

### LVM (Large Vision Model)
**What it is:** A neural network trained on large image-text datasets to understand visual information and make decisions from images or spatial data.
**In this paper:** LVM appears only as a **baseline comparison** for trajectory planning. The paper tests whether an LVM could replace the SOCP-based trajectory optimizer. The SOCP approach outperforms the LVM for UAV trajectory control.
**Easy way to remember:** A computer vision AI attempting to do geometric flight path planning — impressive generalist, but outpaced by purpose-built math tools here.

### FIFO (First In First Out)
**What it is:** A queuing discipline where tasks are processed in the order they arrive. The first task to join the queue is the first to be served.
**In this paper:** Both UAVs and the BS use FIFO scheduling for computation queues. This creates queuing delays — if a vehicle's data arrives while the UAV is still processing a previous vehicle's task, it must wait.
**Easy way to remember:** The line at a coffee shop — first person in line gets served first.

### Alternating Optimization Loop (DRL → LP → LLM → LP)
**What it is:** The specific inner-loop execution sequence in this paper's resource scheduling phase. DRL generates initial allocations; LP solves for offloading ratios and identifies failed/surplus tasks; LLM adjusts allocations; LP solves again with the refined allocations.
**In this paper:** This is the paper's core algorithmic contribution for resource management. The loop runs once per time slot. The DRL reward is recorded after the first LP; the LLM intervention improves final performance without corrupting DRL training.
**Easy way to remember:** Draft → review → revise → finalize — like a document editing workflow, with each step using the best tool for that stage.

## Performance Metrics

### Latency / Delay
**What it is:** The total time from when a vehicle generates a task to when it is fully processed and the result is available. Includes transmission time, queuing time, and computation time.
**In this paper:** The optimization objective jointly minimizes latency and energy. Each vehicle has a task deadline of 1 second. Tasks that exceed this deadline are "failed tasks." Normalized delay (actual delay / deadline) is used to compare tasks with different deadlines fairly.
**Easy way to remember:** How long you wait from clicking "submit" to seeing the result — every component of the pipeline (upload, queue, compute) adds time.

### Energy Consumption
**What it is:** The total electrical energy used by the system across all components: UAV propulsion energy, vehicle transmission energy, UAV and BS computation energy.
**In this paper:** Energy and delay are jointly minimized with weights ω1 (delay) and ω2 (energy). UAV propulsion follows a rotary-wing aerodynamic model with U-shaped power-vs-speed profile (more efficient at moderate speeds than hovering or maximum speed).
**Easy way to remember:** The total fuel bill for running the whole system — flying drones burn power, transmitting data burns power, computing burns power.

### Task Success Rate
**What it is:** The fraction of all vehicle tasks that are completed within their deadlines. The primary performance metric for evaluating how well the system serves vehicles.
**In this paper:** The proposed method significantly outperforms all baselines in task success rate, which is the main headline result. The LLM macro-scheduler specifically improves this metric by rescuing failed tasks that the DRL agent would have abandoned.
**Easy way to remember:** The percentage of customer orders that get delivered on time.

## Network Architecture Terms

### Hierarchical Framework
**What it is:** A system design where different components operate at different levels of abstraction and timescale, with higher levels providing guidance or constraints to lower levels.
**In this paper:** The paper's algorithm is hierarchical: SOCP handles slow-timescale trajectory (physical layer geometry); DRL handles medium-timescale resource scheduling (radio resource management); LLM handles exception-case macro-adjustments; LP handles per-slot offloading ratios. Each layer has a different tool and timescale.
**Easy way to remember:** Like a company hierarchy — CEO sets strategy (LLM), managers allocate teams (DRL), accountants optimize budgets (LP), and engineers execute physical work (SOCP trajectory).

### Trajectory Planning
**What it is:** The problem of deciding the sequence of positions a moving agent should occupy over time to achieve some objective (maximize coverage, minimize energy, etc.) subject to dynamics constraints (speed limits, collision avoidance).
**In this paper:** UAV trajectory planning determines each UAV's 3D position at each time slot. The paper's SOCP-based sequential distributed algorithm plans trajectories by jointly maximizing vehicle coverage and minimizing altitude (to reduce path loss and energy) within kinematic and collision constraints.
**Easy way to remember:** Planning the route your delivery drone should take to visit the most customers while using the least fuel.
