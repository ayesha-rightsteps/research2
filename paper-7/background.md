# Background: Resource Allocation and Sharing for UAV-Assisted Integrated TN-NTN with Multi-Connectivity

## What field does this paper belong to?

This paper belongs to wireless communications engineering, specifically the emerging subfield of integrated terrestrial and non-terrestrial networks (TN-NTN) targeted at 6G systems. It addresses the mathematical optimization problem of jointly allocating spectrum and power resources to heterogeneous UAVs that can simultaneously connect to both ground-based radio base stations and high-altitude platforms. The paper was published as an arXiv preprint in January 2026 and is authored by researchers at Kyung Hee University (South Korea), University of Luxembourg, and Ghent University/NUST Balochistan Campus (Pakistan).

## The Evolution of This Problem

The story of this paper begins with the structural limitation of purely terrestrial cellular networks. Ground-based radio base stations provide excellent coverage in populated areas but leave persistent blind spots in rural regions, over water, and in airspace above the ground-level coverage layer. Satellite networks address some of these gaps but carry high latency and cost, and their large footprints make precise resource allocation difficult. High-altitude platforms — stratospheric aircraft or balloons operating at altitudes around 17-20 km — emerged as an intermediate layer, offering broader coverage than terrestrial cells with lower latency than satellites. The resulting three-tier vision of terrestrial networks, high-altitude platforms, and satellite constellations forms the basis of what 3GPP has been standardizing as integrated TN-NTN for 5G and 6G systems.

UAVs occupy a unique position in this architecture. Operating at low altitudes (typically 0.1-0.5 km), they are low-cost, rapidly deployable, and can establish both upward links to HAPs and downward links to ground RBS simultaneously. This dual connectivity puts UAVs at the intersection of the terrestrial and non-terrestrial segments, making them ideal data mules and relay nodes in integrated TN-NTN. However, the same properties that make UAVs valuable also make their resource management exceptionally complex: they move continuously, their channels fluctuate rapidly due to Doppler shifts and changing propagation geometry, and a UAV swarm contains devices with fundamentally different communication roles — some primarily transfer bulk data (high-capacity role) while others coordinate flight and safety information (local reliability role).

Prior work on resource allocation in UAV networks addressed pieces of this problem in isolation. Studies on cognitive radio-based approaches focused on interference management between UAVs and incumbents, but did not consider the simultaneous presence of HAP links. Multi-agent deep reinforcement learning approaches targeted dynamic channel and power allocation but largely ignored UAV heterogeneity. Research on multi-connectivity (MC) — the 3GPP-standardized capability for a device to maintain simultaneous connections to multiple network nodes — had been applied to UAVs in several contexts, but existing MC-UAV papers uniformly ignored device heterogeneity, differentiated QoS requirements, and the joint optimization of spectrum sharing and power control within an integrated TN-NTN framework. As Table I in the paper documents comprehensively, no prior work simultaneously addressed MC, UAV heterogeneity, differentiated QoS, spectrum sharing, and fairness — all five of which are essential features of practical integrated TN-NTN deployments.

The timing of this gap matters: 3GPP has been actively standardizing NTN features for 5G New Radio, and the transition to 6G networks is projected to massively expand UAV deployment in urban environments. Without a principled framework for joint spectrum and power allocation that accounts for heterogeneous UAV types, MC-enabled networks risk either wasting spectral resources (by over-restricting spectrum reuse to avoid interference) or failing reliability requirements for safety-critical UAV-to-UAV coordination links.

## Key Technologies This Paper Builds On

### Multi-Connectivity (MC) in 3GPP 6G Standards
Multi-connectivity is a 3GPP-standardized feature originally developed for terrestrial reliability enhancement, now being extended to NTN. An MC-capable device maintains simultaneous active connections to multiple network nodes — in this paper's model, an HCU UAV is connected to both an RBS (terrestrial link) and a HAP (non-terrestrial link) at the same time. The paper demonstrates that MC provides substantial capacity gains over single-connectivity (SC) across all evaluated configurations, validating its role as a foundational enabler for integrated TN-NTN.

### Large-Scale Fading Channel Modeling with Rayleigh Small-Scale Fading
The channel model in this paper combines three components: deterministic path loss (log-distance model), log-normal shadowing (random variable chi_{x,y}), and Rayleigh small-scale fading (exponential-distributed power gain g^(s)). The critical design choice is to rely only on large-scale parameters (path loss + shadowing) for resource allocation decisions, averaging out the small-scale fading in ergodic capacity calculations. This is justified because large-scale fading evolves only after UAV displacements of tens of meters, corresponding to update intervals of hundreds of milliseconds — long enough for practical centralized optimization — while instantaneous small-scale CSI would require infeasibly rapid updates for fast-moving UAVs.

### Hungarian Method (HM) for Assignment Problems
The Hungarian Method is a classical combinatorial optimization algorithm that solves the linear assignment problem — optimally matching a set of agents to a set of tasks — in polynomial time O(n^3). This paper exploits the HM as a core computational tool: once optimal per-pair power allocations are determined, the spectrum sharing pattern (which HCU shares its band with which LCU) is formulated as an assignment problem solvable by the HM. This gives the paper's algorithms polynomial-time complexity, making them tractable for networks with tens of UAVs.

## Why This Paper's Approach is Different

The central novelty is a two-step optimization framework that decouples power allocation from spectrum sharing pattern design, enabling efficient joint optimization through sequential analytical and combinatorial steps. In the first step, the paper derives a closed-form expression for the maximum HCU capacity achievable for each candidate HCU-LCU spectrum-sharing pair, given the LCU's reliability constraint. This per-pair optimal power solution is obtained analytically using a monotonicity argument and a bisection search on a derived constraint function f(P^l_j). In the second step, these per-pair maximum capacities are assembled into a capacity matrix, and the HM finds the optimal one-to-one pairing that maximizes either total throughput (Algorithm 1) or the minimum per-HCU capacity (Algorithm 2).

The max-min optimization of Algorithm 2 is particularly notable: by framing fairness as maximizing the worst-case capacity, it guarantees Pareto-optimal outcomes and is solved through a binary-search-guided HM approach that iterates over the discrete set of candidate minimum capacities. The paper shows that these algorithms outperform both no-spectrum-sharing baselines and greedy sharing approaches under all tested configurations of J/I ratio, UAV speed, and LCU SINR thresholds, while maintaining polynomial-time computational complexity.

## Where Does This Paper Sit in the Bigger Picture?

This paper positions itself as foundational infrastructure work for 6G integrated TN-NTN. It provides the first framework that simultaneously addresses all five practical requirements — MC, heterogeneity, differentiated QoS, spectrum sharing, and fairness — and derives algorithms with provable convergence and polynomial complexity. The practical implications are concrete: Algorithm 1 enables smart city bulk-data applications where aggregate throughput matters more than individual fairness; Algorithm 2 enables healthcare or safety-critical UAV coordination where every link must meet a guaranteed minimum quality. Looking forward, the paper explicitly identifies extensions needed for full deployment readiness: more complex interference scenarios with many-to-one spectrum sharing, explicit Doppler-domain modeling using OTFS waveforms, dynamic handover mechanisms as UAVs move between RBS cells, and trajectory optimization coupled with resource allocation. This places the paper at the frontier of a rapidly maturing field where mathematical tractability must be reconciled with the messy dynamics of real UAV swarms operating in urban airspace.
